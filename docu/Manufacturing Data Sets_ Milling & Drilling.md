

# **An Expert Review of Public Datasets for Condition Monitoring and Prognostics in Milling and Drilling Operations**

## **Introduction to Data-Driven PHM in Machining Processes**

The contemporary manufacturing landscape is undergoing a profound transformation, driven by the principles of Industry 4.0. This evolution marks a definitive shift away from traditional reactive and time-based preventive maintenance strategies towards predictive, data-driven paradigms.1 At the heart of this transformation lies the engineering discipline of Prognostics and Health Management (PHM), a comprehensive framework dedicated to monitoring system health, detecting and diagnosing failures, and forecasting the future operational capability of components and systems.3 The core tasks within PHM—fault detection, diagnostics, and prognostics—are fundamental to enhancing operational efficiency, minimizing unscheduled downtime, improving product quality, and ensuring the safety of industrial machinery.2 The availability of public datasets is not merely a convenience but a critical catalyst for this field; these shared benchmarks have historically spurred algorithmic innovation and provided a common ground for evaluating and comparing new prognostic methods, thereby accelerating the entire research and development cycle.6  
The efficacy of any PHM system is contingent upon the acquisition and analysis of high-fidelity, multivariate time-series data from the shop floor.1 These data streams, captured by an array of sensors, provide invaluable insights into the underlying degradation processes and evolving health conditions of manufacturing equipment. Vibration signals, typically captured by accelerometers, are of paramount importance as they directly reflect the complex, high-frequency dynamics of the tool-workpiece interaction, revealing phenomena such as tool chatter, imbalance, and changes in cutting stability.8 However, the most robust PHM solutions often leverage a multi-modal sensing approach. Comprehensive datasets frequently supplement vibration data with other critical signals, including cutting forces measured by dynamometers, which quantify the energy of material removal; acoustic emissions (AE), which are sensitive to microscopic events like crack initiation and friction; and spindle motor currents, which serve as a proxy for the load on the system.10 This fusion of sensor modalities provides a more holistic and resilient characterization of the machining process.  
To develop and validate predictive models, these sensor data streams must be paired with a clear and quantifiable anomaly measure, which serves as the "ground truth" or target variable. In the context of machining, these prognostic targets manifest in several forms. The most common is a continuous measure of **tool wear**, often quantified as the width of the flank wear land (VB), which provides a direct physical measurement of the tool's degradation over time.13 Another critical target, particularly for prognostic tasks, is the  
**Remaining Useful Life (RUL)**, defined as the remaining time or number of operational cycles before a component is expected to fail or cease performing its intended function.3 Finally, for many practical applications, the anomaly is represented as a  
**categorical fault**, which can be a binary classification (e.g., 'worn' versus 'unworn') or a multi-class health state (e.g., 'healthy', 'moderate wear', 'critical wear').10 The selection of a dataset is therefore a strategic decision, dictated not only by the available sensor data but also by the nature of the ground truth labels and their alignment with the specific PHM task at hand.

### **The NASA Prognostics Repository Milling Dataset (UC Berkeley BEST Lab)**

This dataset, provided by the UC Berkeley Emergent Space Tensegrities (BEST) Lab and hosted by the NASA Prognostics Center of Excellence (PCoE), stands as one of the foundational public resources in the field.13 It was created to spur the development of prognostic algorithms by providing a well-documented set of run-to-failure data.

* **Experimental Context and Machining Parameters:** The experiments encompass 16 distinct cases of milling operations performed on two different workpiece materials: cast iron and steel. The cutting parameters were systematically varied across these cases, with two levels for feed rate (0.25 mm/rev and 0.5 mm/rev) and two levels for depth of cut (0.75 mm and 1.5 mm). This structured variation allows researchers to study the influence of operating conditions on tool degradation and sensor signals.14  
* **Sensor Data Profile:** The dataset is explicitly multi-modal, providing a rich source for sensor fusion research. For each of the 165 recorded cuts, six channels of time-series data were collected at a sampling rate of 250 Hz. These channels include vibration from both the spindle and the machine table, acoustic emission from the spindle and table, and both AC and DC current from the spindle motor. Each signal recording has a total length of 36 seconds, containing 9000 sample points.14  
* **Degradation Metric:** The primary anomaly and degradation measure is the flank wear (VB) of the milling tool inserts. This physical measurement was taken for most of the cuts, providing a continuous degradation signal that tracks the tool's health from a new state to a fully worn state. This makes the dataset highly suitable for training and validating regression models aimed at predicting the precise level of tool wear.13  
* **Data Structure and Access:** The dataset is available for download from the NASA Prognostics Data Repository.13 The original data is organized in a structured MATLAB array, which is convenient for academic researchers familiar with that environment.14 It is worth noting that a simulated version of this dataset also exists on Kaggle, which may offer easier access for some users but whose data generation process and characteristics might differ from the original experimental data.22 Due to its status as a benchmark, this dataset has been used in numerous research papers to validate novel approaches to tool wear prediction.24

### **The 2010 PHM Society Data Challenge: A Benchmark for RUL Estimation**

The dataset from the 2010 PHM Society Data Challenge is arguably the most influential and widely used benchmark for the specific task of Remaining Useful Life (RUL) estimation in milling. Its origin as a formal competition ensures that the prognostic problem is well-defined, the data is standardized, and the performance metrics are clear.17

* **Experimental Setup:** The experiments were conducted on a Röders Tech RFM760 high-speed CNC milling machine, using a 6mm three-flute ball-nose tungsten carbide cutter to machine a workpiece of Inconel 718, a high-strength, hard-to-machine superalloy.17 The machining parameters were fixed and are precisely documented: a spindle speed of 10,400 RPM, a feed rate of 1555 mm/min, a radial depth of cut of 0.125 mm, and an axial depth of cut of 0.2 mm.17 The use of a challenging material under high-speed conditions makes the degradation signals particularly rich and relevant for aerospace and other high-performance manufacturing applications.  
* **Multi-Modal Sensor Data Profile:** This dataset is distinguished by its exceptionally high-fidelity sensor data. Seven channels were recorded simultaneously at a sampling rate of 50 kHz per channel. This includes triaxial cutting forces (Fx, Fy, Fz) from a Kistler dynamometer, triaxial vibration (Vx, Vy, Vz) from a Kistler accelerometer, and the root-mean-square of an acoustic emission signal (AE-RMS).12 The high sampling rate is crucial for capturing the transient, high-frequency phenomena associated with tool wear and failure modes.  
* **Prognostic Target:** The dataset is structured for a supervised learning task. It is divided into a training set (data from cutters c1, c4, and c6) and a test set (c2, c3, and c5). For the training cutters, a corresponding "wear" file provides the measured flank wear in micrometers (10^-3 mm) after each cut. The explicit objective of the challenge was to use the sensor data from the training set to build a model that could accurately predict the RUL of the cutters in the test set.17  
* **Data Access:** While the original links on the PHM Society website may be unstable, the dataset's importance has led to its preservation on multiple platforms, including Kaggle and various university-hosted archives, ensuring its continued accessibility for the research community.17

### **Datasets from Public Data Science Platforms (Kaggle & UCI)**

Data science platforms like Kaggle and the UCI Machine Learning Repository host several valuable datasets that lower the barrier to entry for researchers and practitioners. These datasets often feature pre-processed data and are associated with a community of users who share code and analysis.

#### **University of Michigan SMART Lab Dataset ("CNC Mill Tool Wear")**

This dataset, contributed by shasun on Kaggle, originates from the System-level Manufacturing and Automation Research Testbed (SMART) at the University of Michigan.16 It presents a unique approach to condition monitoring by relying on readily available machine control data rather than specialized external sensors.

* **Experimental Setup and Data:** The dataset comprises 18 experiments involving the milling of an "S" shape into wax blocks. The experiments were conducted with variations in tool condition, feed rate, and workpiece clamping pressure.16 The time-series data, sampled at 10 Hz (100 ms interval), does not include direct vibration or force measurements. Instead, it contains signals from the CNC machine's four servo motors: X, Y, Z axes, and the spindle. These signals include  
  ActualPosition, ActualVelocity, and ActualAcceleration, which serve as indirect or proxy measurements of the process state.16 This approach is highly relevant for industrial applications on legacy equipment where installing external sensors may not be feasible.  
* **Anomaly Measure:** The primary ground truth is a binary classification label, tool\_condition, which is either 'unworn' or 'worn'.16 This makes the dataset ideal for developing and benchmarking classification algorithms. It also includes secondary labels related to workpiece quality (  
  passed\_visual\_inspection) and process completion, which can be used for multi-task learning or to study the relationship between tool health and product quality.16 This dataset is frequently cited in research exploring deep learning models, such as hybrid CNN-LSTMs with attention mechanisms, for tool wear classification.30

#### **Multi-Sensor CNC Tool Wear Dataset**

This Kaggle dataset, from user ziya07, is explicitly designed for multi-sensor tool wear monitoring and directly addresses the core requirements of the user query.10

* **Sensor Data:** Unlike the U. Michigan dataset, this one provides data derived directly from dedicated process sensors. It contains features that have been extracted from triaxial cutting force signals (Fx, Fy, Fz), triaxial vibration signals (X, Y, Z), and acoustic emission (AE) signals.10 By providing pre-extracted features rather than raw time-series data, this dataset allows researchers to focus on the machine learning aspect of the problem without needing deep expertise in signal processing.  
* **Anomaly Measure:** A key strength of this dataset is its versatility in terms of the target variable. It provides both a continuous target, VB\_mm, representing the flank wear in millimeters for regression tasks, and a categorical target, Wear\_Class, with three levels ('Healthy', 'Moderate', 'Worn') for multi-class classification tasks.10

#### **The UCI Bosch CNC Machining Dataset**

This dataset is particularly valuable because it bridges the gap between controlled laboratory experiments and the complexities of real-world manufacturing. It was collected from a "brownfield" production environment over a two-year period, capturing the inherent variability, noise, and process drifts that are often absent in academic datasets.18

* **Sensor Data:** The data consists of tri-axial acceleration (vibration) measurements collected from a smart sensing system installed on the milling machines.18 The data was collected from three different CNC machines, each running 15 different processes, providing a basis for studying model scalability and process variations.33  
* **Anomaly Measure:** The dataset is labeled to differentiate between "normal" and "abnormal" process executions (labeled as "good" and "bad"). This makes it an excellent resource for developing and testing anomaly detection algorithms designed for deployment in industrial settings, where the primary task is often to flag any deviation from a healthy baseline.18  
* **Data Access:** The dataset is archived in the UCI Machine Learning Repository and is also available via a GitHub repository, which includes detailed documentation and the associated research paper describing the data collection methodology.18

The existence of these distinct dataset types—from the clean, controlled laboratory setting of the U. Michigan experiments to the noisy, variable "brownfield" environment of the Bosch data—creates a spectrum of complexity. A model that achieves near-perfect accuracy on the wax-milling dataset might fail entirely when deployed on the Bosch data due to the domain shift. This highlights a critical area of research: the development of algorithms that are robust and can generalize from controlled conditions to the factory floor. Datasets like Bosch's are indispensable for validating this robustness, while others, as discussed next, are designed to explore the intermediate step of transferring knowledge between different machines.

### **Advanced Research Datasets from Academic Repositories (Mendeley)**

#### **The Denkena et al. Multivariate Milling Dataset**

Published in 2023, this dataset is a modern and sophisticated resource specifically designed to address a key challenge in industrial PHM: transfer learning.19 In practice, a model trained on data from one machine often performs poorly on another, even if it is the same model. This dataset provides the necessary data to research and develop domain adaptation techniques to overcome this problem.

* **Experimental Setup:** The experiments were conducted on three different 5-axis milling centers. Critically, the workpieces (cast iron 600-3/S), experimental setups, and NC programs were kept identical across all three machines. Nine solid carbide end milling cutters were used, with each tool run from a new state to its end of life (defined as VB≈150μm).19  
* **Sensor Data:** This dataset does not contain direct vibration data from external accelerometers. Instead, it offers two sets of synchronized data streams. The first is high-frequency (25 kHz) process force data from a Kistler dynamometer. The second is lower-frequency (500 Hz) data read directly from the machine tool's controller, which includes signals like the force or torque of the spindle and feed drives, and the position control deviation of the feed axes.19  
* **Degradation Metric:** The ground truth for degradation is the width of the flank wear land (VB), which was frequently measured using a digital microscope. The dataset contains a total of 6,418 labeled data files, with each file corresponding to a single milling run and labeled with the machine ID, tool ID, run number, and the corresponding VB value in micrometers.19

### **The Mendeley Drilling Tool Wear Dataset (Nair & Sakthivel)**

This dataset provides a focused collection of sensor data from a drilling process, designed to capture the evolution of tool wear from a new to a failed state.40

* **Experimental Design:** The experimental campaign involved four separate drilling tools. Each tool was run through a series of drilling operations until it was considered blunt (a run-to-failure experiment). For each tool, the process was divided into two distinct runs (R01 and R02). A visual inspection of the tool for wear was conducted between the first and second run, providing a qualitative midpoint check on the degradation process.40  
* **Sensor Data Profile:** The dataset directly meets the user's primary requirement for vibration data. The primary sensor was a Dytran 3-axis accelerometer (Model 3273A2), with data collected at a sampling rate of 1000 Hz (1 kHz). In addition to acceleration, the dataset also includes signals for RPM, torque, and feed force, providing a multi-modal view of the drilling operation.40  
* **Degradation Metric:** This dataset presents an interesting case regarding the ground truth for tool wear. It does not provide continuous, quantitative measurements of flank wear at frequent intervals. Instead, the degradation is represented implicitly through the experimental structure. The data files are clearly labeled by tool and run number (e.g., DrillingToolWearDataset\_T01\_R01.xlsx). This labeling effectively creates distinct health stages: data from R01 represents a "new" or "healthy" tool, while data from R02 represents a "worn" tool progressing to a "blunt" or "failed" state.40 This structure is common in industrial data collection, where frequent process interruptions for precise measurements are impractical.  
* **Data Access and Structure:** The data is hosted on the Mendeley Data repository and is provided in easily accessible Excel (.xlsx) format. The clear and consistent file naming convention simplifies data organization and analysis.40 The lack of continuous wear labels makes this dataset less suitable for training standard supervised regression models. However, it is exceptionally well-suited for other critical PHM tasks, such as developing unsupervised anomaly detection models (training on "new" data from Run 1 and testing on "worn" data from Run 2\) or for training classifiers to distinguish between different health stages of the drilling tool.

## **Synthesis and Strategic Recommendations for Dataset Selection**

The choice of a dataset is a foundational decision that shapes the scope, methodology, and potential impact of any research in data-driven PHM. No single dataset is universally superior; the optimal choice is intrinsically linked to the specific research question being addressed. This section provides a synthesized analysis and a strategic guide for selecting the most appropriate dataset based on common research objectives.

### **Comparative Analysis for Specific Research Tasks**

The reviewed datasets are each uniquely suited for different tasks within the PHM spectrum. The principle that the research task defines the necessary data is paramount.

* **For RUL Regression:** The **2010 PHM Society Data Challenge** dataset is the unequivocal gold standard. Its high-frequency, multi-modal sensor data, combined with a clear RUL objective and wear measurements for training, provides the ideal environment for developing and benchmarking sophisticated regression models for RUL estimation.17 The  
  **NASA Milling Dataset** is also a strong candidate, as its continuous flank wear (VB) measurements can be readily converted into a RUL target.13  
* **For Wear State Classification:** For binary classification ('worn'/'unworn'), the **University of Michigan Dataset** offers a unique challenge: classifying tool state using indirect motor signals, a scenario highly relevant to legacy industrial equipment.16 For multi-class classification ('Healthy'/'Moderate'/'Worn'), the  
  **Multi-Sensor CNC Tool Wear Dataset** is an excellent choice, providing feature-engineered data from direct sensors that is ready for use with a wide range of classification algorithms.10  
* **For Industrial Anomaly Detection:** The **UCI Bosch CNC Machining Dataset** is the most suitable for this task. Its origin in a real "brownfield" production environment means it contains the noise, process drift, and unmodeled dynamics that are the primary challenges for robust anomaly detection in practice. A model that performs well on this dataset is more likely to be viable for real-world deployment.18  
* **For Transfer Learning Research:** The **Denkena et al. Milling Dataset** was explicitly created for this purpose. With data from three different machine tools running identical processes, it allows researchers to directly investigate and validate domain adaptation and transfer learning techniques, which are crucial for creating scalable PHM solutions.19  
* **For Drilling Applications:** The **Mendeley Drilling Dataset** is the primary public resource. Due to its run-to-failure structure with sparse ground truth, it is best suited for developing models for health stage classification (new vs. worn) or for unsupervised anomaly detection algorithms.40

### **The Multi-Modal Data Advantage**

While the initial query focused on vibration data, a consistent theme across the most valuable datasets is the availability of multi-modal sensor streams. Relying solely on vibration can be limiting, as different physical phenomena during machining manifest in different signal domains. The most powerful and robust prognostic models are invariably built on the principle of sensor fusion.  
For instance, in the PHM 2010 dataset, vibration signals are excellent for capturing high-frequency dynamics like tool chatter, but the force signals provide a more direct measure of the energy being expended to remove material. Acoustic emission, in turn, is uniquely sensitive to the microscopic stress waves generated by material fracture and friction at the cutting interface.11 By fusing information from these complementary sources, a model can construct a far more comprehensive and reliable assessment of the tool's health. Datasets like the  
**PHM 2010 Challenge** and the **NASA Milling Dataset** are therefore more valuable for advanced research than single-sensor datasets because they enable the development and validation of these critical sensor fusion strategies.14

### **Identifying Gaps and Future Directions**

This comprehensive review also highlights several gaps in the current landscape of public manufacturing datasets, pointing toward opportunities for future data collection campaigns.

* **Scarcity of Drilling Data:** There is a clear and significant disparity in the availability of public data for milling versus drilling. The development of more comprehensive, multi-sensor, run-to-failure datasets for various drilling operations would be a major contribution to the field.  
* **Limited Material and Tool Variety:** The majority of existing datasets focus on a narrow range of tool-workpiece combinations, typically carbide tools on steel or superalloys. There is a pressing need for datasets that cover a broader spectrum of industrially relevant scenarios, including the machining of composite materials (e.g., CFRP), plastics, and other alloys, as well as the performance of different tool geometries and coatings.42  
* **Integration of Workpiece Quality Metrics:** Most datasets focus exclusively on the health of the tool or machine. However, the ultimate goal of manufacturing is to produce a quality part. Future datasets should aim to close the loop between process monitoring and quality control by integrating metrology data from the finished workpiece, such as surface roughness measurements and dimensional accuracy assessments. This would enable the development of models that predict not just tool failure, but also the degradation of product quality.  
* **Data from Complex Machining Operations:** The available datasets are predominantly from relatively simple processes like 3-axis shoulder milling or single-pass drilling. To test the robustness and scalability of PHM models, there is a need for data from more complex operations, such as 5-axis machining, high-speed trochoidal milling, or processes involving automated tool changes and multiple sequential operations.
